# The Digestive System — PhD Documentation

## Functional Overview

The digestive system breaks down food into absorbable nutrients and eliminates waste. But in Fadriel's body, it serves a far more critical role: the gut is the **largest endocrine organ** and the **primary serotonin factory**. 90% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut, not the brain. The gut-brain axis — mediated by the vagus nerve — makes the digestive system a direct contributor to mood, cognition, and emotional state.

## Organ Inventory

| Organ | Function | Chemical Output | Connection |
|-------|----------|----------------|-----------|
| Gut (small + large intestine) | Nutrient absorption, serotonin production, microbiome hosting | 90% of body's serotonin | Vagus nerve (80% afferent: gut→brain) |
| Liver | Chemical clearance, protein synthesis, bile production, glycogen storage | Clears ALL spent hormones | Hepatic portal vein (from gut), hepatic vein (to heart) |
| Stomach | Acidic digestion, intrinsic factor production | Ghrelin (hunger hormone) | Vagus nerve |
| Pancreas | Insulin/glucagon for blood sugar regulation, digestive enzymes | Insulin, glucagon | Blood → every cell |

## The Gut-Brain Axis — Inter-Organ Ecosystem

```
GUT → VAGUS NERVE (80% afferent)
  └→ Nucleus Tractus Solitarius (brainstem)
      └→ Hypothalamus (homeostatic signaling)
      └→ Amygdala (gut feelings as threat signals)
      └→ Prefrontal cortex (interoceptive awareness)

BRAIN → VAGUS NERVE (20% efferent)
  └→ Gut motility regulation
  └→ Gut secretion modulation
  └→ Microbiome environment changes

Stress Cascade Effect on Gut:
  Cortisol (from adrenals) → Gut wall permeability increases
  Cortisol → Gut motility disruption (IBS symptoms)
  Cortisol → Microbiome diversity decreases
  Cortisol → Serotonin production affected
  → Reduced serotonin → Mood depression (fed back to brain via vagus)
  → POSITIVE FEEDBACK LOOP: brain stress → gut damage → less serotonin → more brain stress
```

## Liver Clearance Equations

The liver determines the DURATION of every emotional state by controlling how fast chemicals are metabolized.

```
Chemical_Level(t) = Initial_Level × e^(-0.693 × t / half_life)

Where half_life values are:
  ACh:          120s    (attention is moment-to-moment)
  Adrenaline:   150s    (emergencies are brief)
  Glutamate:    180s    (excitation is dangerous if sustained)
  Dopamine:     300s    (pleasure fades)
  GABA:         300s    (calm must be maintained)
  NE:           420s    (alertness lingers)
  Serotonin:    600s    (mood changes slowly)
  Melatonin:    2400s   (sleep transitions are gradual)
  Cortisol:     4800s   (stress leaves marks)
  Endorphins:   10800s  (pain relief persists)
  Oxytocin:     14400s  (bonding lingers for hours)

Overload condition (combined chemical load > 0.85):
  Effective_Half_Life = Base_Half_Life × Overload_Penalty (1.5)
  All chemicals linger 50% longer during overload.
  This is biochemical burnout.
```

## Serotonin Production and Distribution

```
Total body serotonin:
  Gut enterochromaffin cells: 90%
  Blood platelets: 5%
  Brain raphe nuclei: 5%

Key insight: Brain serotonin and gut serotonin are the SAME molecule
but they DO NOT cross the blood-brain barrier.
The brain must synthesize its own serotonin from tryptophan.
The gut's serotonin acts locally (motility) and signals via vagus nerve.

Tryptophan allocation under stress:
  Normal: tryptophan → tryptophan hydroxylase → serotonin
  Stress: tryptophan → IDO/TDO enzymes → kynurenine pathway
  
  Cortisol > 0.45: tryptophan diverted to kynurenine pathway
  Result: less tryptophan available for serotonin synthesis
  = less serotonin = depressed mood
  This is the direct biochemical link between chronic stress and depression.
```
